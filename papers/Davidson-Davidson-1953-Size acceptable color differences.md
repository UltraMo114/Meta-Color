# The Size of Acceptable Color Differences

HUGH R. DAVIDSON, * Central Research Laboratory, General Aniline and Film Corporation, Easton, Pennsylvania

AND

ELAINE FRIEDE, Alexander Smith, Inc., Yonkers, New York (Received December 2, 1952)

In order to determine the size of acceptable color differences for a particular production problem, about 12 000 individual judgements of the acceptability of 287 samples as matches with 19 standards were made. The samples represented colors in all sections of the color domain, and the samples represented various size color differences from the standards. Colorimetric measurements of the samples were made and the color differences were calculated according to the several methods currently in use. Color differences were then plotted against the visual judgements of acceptability. Calculations based on rather small modifications of the MacAdam ellipses were found to correlate with the visual results much better than do those based on other methods. We found that color differences falling within an ellipsoid about 2½ times the size of those published by MacAdam with a radius vector of about 0.15 Munsell value unit parallel to the Y axis were accepted in 50 percent of the observations.

## I NTRODUCTION

COLOR tolerance specifications might be established by means of material standards illustrating the allowable departures in color from a standard, by means of three numerical values representing the allowable departure in three color attributes, or by means of a single value representing the allowable departure in any possible combination of attributes. Difficulties with material standards and visual comparisons are well known and have prompted investigation of the other possible means. The second means has proved successful in certain production problems, and has been considered by many workers in this field to be the only practical method. The disadvantages of this method are, first, the requirement of three numbers for specification and some means of combining them for composite color differences and, second, the need for establishing each of the three tolerances empirically for each region of the color domain. Refinements in this method lead logically to the third means, use of a single number specification.

There are several obstacles in the way of achieving a single number tolerance system, the most important of which may best be expressed in the simple question, is it possible? This question has been the basis of many arguments among colorimetrists, but very little experimental evidence has been available to buttress either side in the dispute. The objectives of the study to be reported here are to answer this question for one particular set of experimental conditions and to determine which of the many suggested methods of color difference calculation shows most promise of being generally applicable for color tolerance specification.

## EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE

A series of 19 colors on wool flannel, designated "standards" in this paper, were chosen as being representative of the color domain. An average of 15 wool flannel samples differing in various color attributes from each of the standards were then chosen as representative of color differences encountered in industrial problems. Thus, a total of 19 standards and 287 samples were prepared for visual examination. Each sample and standard consisted of a two-inch square of material stapled to a white cardboard of slightly smaller dimensions. The samples were identified by a number on the cardboard which was not visible to the observer, and the standards were identified for the observer by a small strip of black paper along one side of the square. The same dyestuffs were used for both standard and sample dyeings in order to eliminate any effects due to metamerism.

The objectives of this study, it should be noted, led us to employ conditions for our experiment which closely approximate those found in carpet yarn production. No precautions of any kind were taken to insure uniformity of background and angle of viewing. No precautions were taken to reduce fatigue or to prevent sizes of color differences in one series from influencing the judgements in another series. In other words, in this study we adopted few of the many psychological precautions which ought to be taken to insure general validity of the data. These precautions were neglected simply because in the actual production problems of interest to us, they

are normally neglected. This means, of course, that our conclusions are strictly valid only for our particular problem.

All samples and standards were measured several times with the recording spectrophotometer and automatic tristimulus integrator. The standard deviation of measurement errors appears to be less than 0.0005 in trichromatic coordinates and 0.0005 in the Y tristimulus value. Colorimetric specifications of the standards are given in Table I, and the differences between standard and sample specifications are given in Table II. The Munsell renotation tables were used in obtaining Munsell notations. $ ^{1} $ 

## RESULTS

The “percent acceptance” of each sample was first established simply by dividing the number of observations in which the sample was accepted by the total number of observations made and multiplying by 100. When the percent acceptance was computed separately for two sets of data taken several months apart, it was found that the values remained about the same except for the small fluctuations to be expected from this sort of statistical data. The average difference between each set of observations and the mean of the two sets was about 7 percent acceptance. The average of the two sets of observations is given under the heading “percent acceptability” in Table II.

The colorimetric specifications given in Table II were plotted in an xy diagram and an xY diagram. Symmetrical ellipses of such a size as to include as nearly as possible all samples accepted in 50 percent of the observations and to exclude all others were then drawn about the standard. When these had been completed we noted a striking resemblance between our ellipses and those of MacAdam. $ ^{2} $  Our ellipses were of nearly the same shape and orientation as MacAdam's, but were about  $ 2\frac{1}{2} $  times larger. We found that the third dimension of our ellipsoids, i.e., the dimension parallel to the Y axis, was equal to about 0.15 Munsell value unit. These results seemed to indicate that color differences calculated from MacAdam ellipses $ ^{2} $  would correlate well with the visual data.

Color differences between samples and standards were made, using seven different formulas. Five of the formulas used were those due to Adams, $ ^{4} $  Godlove (Modified Adams), $ ^{5} $  Godlove (Munsell), $ ^{6} $  Judd, $ ^{7} $  and Nickerson. $ ^{8} $  The sixth was the formula

 $$ \Delta E=\left[\frac{g_{11}d x^{2}+2g_{12}d x d y+g_{22}d y^{2}}{(2.5)^{2}}+\frac{(d V)^{2}}{(0.15)^{2}}\right]^{\frac{1}{2}}. $$ 

This is equivalent to that used by MacAdam $ ^{9} $  except that it is based on a unit  $ 2\frac{1}{2} $  times the size of his, and the third dimension in terms of dV (difference in Munsell “value” between sample and standard) has been added. The values for  $ g_{11} $ ,  $ 2g_{12} $ , and  $ g_{22} $  are given by MacAdam as functions of x and y. The seventh equation was one employing the graphical methods described in a previous paper $ ^{3} $  but based on ellipses somewhat altered from those of MacAdam. This alteration will be described below. The results of calculations based on these equations are recorded in Table III. A multiplying constant has been used in order to obtain approximately the same size unit for all equations, and these constants are given at the head of the columns of Table III.

Finally, the percent acceptances of all samples were plotted against the color differences as obtained by the various formulas. Figures 1 through 8 are the resulting plots for each of the seven equations. The curve in each plot has been drawn on the basis of the average percent acceptance at the indicated color differences. It will be noted that the average curve in each plot indicates that a color difference of 1 unit is accepted in about 50 percent of the observations. This, of course, has been brought about by choice of the multiplying constants for each equation.

Neglecting Figs. 7 and 8 for the moment, it is quite apparent that calculations based on the MacAdam ellipses correlate with the visual data better than do calculations based on the other methods examined. $ ^{[10]} $  Not only is the scatter of points less in Fig. 6 than in Figs. 1 through 5, but the average curve drops to 0 percent acceptance more rapidly at large color differences. Even in the case of Fig. 6, however, the  $ X^{2} $  test shows the scatter to be much larger than we should expect on statistical grounds alone. I. H. Godlove pointed out that part of this scatter is due to an effect of the average size color difference in a series on the size color difference in that series accepted in 50 percent of the observations. Where the average color difference for the series is larger, the size color difference considered acceptable is also larger. A useful correction for this effect can be made by multiplying the calculated color differences in a series by the ratio of the average color difference for all series to the average color difference in the series being corrected. The reduction in scatter brought about by this correction may be evaluated by comparing Fig. 6, where no correction has been made, with the corrected values of Fig. 7. The scatter in Figs. 1 through 5 should also, of course, be reduced by a comparable amount. In spite of this correction, the scatter in Fig. 7 is still much too large to be ascribed to statistical fluctuations.

## CONCLUSIONS

We believe the most important conclusion to be drawn from our study is the fact that excellent correlation may be obtained between the acceptability of a color difference and its size as calculated from visual sensitivity data. This means that it should be possible to set up classes of color tolerance as described above. Until further data confirming the ellipsoids specified by Figs. 9 and 10 are obtained, we believe the graphical

Although there is a possibility that our observers were actually estimating the number of “perceptible” color steps in a given difference rather than the acceptability of the difference, we see no evidence that they did so. In fact, we have always found it much more difficult to obtain a colorist’s opinion of the number of “perceptible” steps of color difference than to obtain his opinion as to whether the difference is acceptable.

In conclusion, we wish to emphasize the fact that the data obtained in this study were taken under the poorly controlled conditions found in most industrial practice in the textile field. Further data of this sort obtained under different conditions are required before we can be certain of the general validity of our results.
