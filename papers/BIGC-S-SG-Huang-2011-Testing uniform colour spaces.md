# Testing Uniform Colour Spaces and Colour-Difference Formulae Using Printed Samples

Min Huang, $ ^{1} $  Haoxue Liu, $ ^{1} $  Guihua Cui, $ ^{2} $  M. Ronnier Luo $ ^{2*} $ 

 $ ^{1} $ School of Printing & Packaging Engineering, Beijing Institute of Graphic Communication, Beijing, China

 $ ^{2} $ Colour, Imaging and Design Research Center, University of Leeds, Leeds, United Kingdom

Received 10 December 2010; revised 11 January 2011; accepted 13 January 2011

Abstract: A grey-scale psychophysical experiment was carried out for evaluating colour differences using printed colour patches. In total, 446 pairs of printed samples were prepared surrounding 17 colour centers recommended by the CIE with an average  $ \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $  of 3 units. Each pair was assessed 27 times by nine observers. The visual results were used to test some selected more advanced colour-difference formulae and uniform colour spaces. The results showed that CIELAB and OSA performed the worst, and the advanced formulae and spaces gave quite satisfactory performance such as CIEDE2000, CIE94, DIN99d, CAM02-UCS, and OSA-GP-Eu. The colour discrimination ellipses were used to compare with those of the earlier studies. The results showed that they agreed well with each other. © 2011 Wiley Periodicals, Inc. ## I NTRODUCTION

Colour-difference evaluation is important for industrial quality control. To achieve this, reliable colour-difference formulae and uniform colour spaces are required. Regarding the latter, various spaces have been developed, such as CIE-LAB, $ ^{1} $  DIN99d, $ ^{2} $  OSA, $ ^{3} $  and CIECAM02. $ ^{4} $  CIELAB is still currently recommended by the CIE as uniform colour space together with CIELUV. $ ^{1} $  The former has been widely used in industry for colour specification and colour-difference evaluation, but it is well known that it does not perform well for different datasets having the medium colour differences, typically with  $ \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $  less than five. Although many colour-difference formulae including the most recent CIEDE2000 $ ^{5} $  were developed based on the modification of CIELAB, they do not have their own associated colour space.

With this in mind, CIE TC 1-55: Uniform colour space for industrial colour-difference evaluation was formed in 1999. It is aimed to propose a uniform colour space to replace CIELAB. Although it was stated that the new space should fit well to the four colour discrimination datasets used to develop CIEDE2000, some reliable new datasets are still welcome to be used for testing the new CIE space. $ ^{6} $  The CIE TC 1-55 suggested that colour pairs should be assessed by a considerable number of observers with normal colour vision under well controlled experimental conditions and using rigid experimental methods.

At the Beijing Institute of Graphic Communication (BIGC), a large research program has been established to evaluate the colour differences for graphic art applications, i.e., all based on printed samples. It has three goals: (1) to establish a comprehensive colour discrimination dataset with an average of medium size, (2) to investigate the colour discrimination between different gloss levels, and (3) to understand the characteristics for assessing colour difference having different magnitudes. This work reports the results from the first goal.

This article describes a new dataset which was produced to fulfill the requirement of CIE TC1-55. The visual experiment was carried out using printed colour patches with a mean colour-difference magnitude of  $ 3.0 \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $  units.

## EXPERIMENTAL

## Sample Preparation and Selection

A large number of samples, surrounding 17 colour centers recommended by the CIE, $ ^{8} $  were generated by an EPSON Stylus PRO 7800 ink-jet printer. ## V isual Assessments

The visual assessments of colour difference were conducted in a dark room using a GretagMacbeth Judge II viewing cabinet equipped with a D65 simulator, which had a correlated colour temperature of 6587 K and an illuminance value of 958 lx measured by a PhotoResearch PR-650 spectroradiometer. The grey background had  $ L_{10}^{*} $ ,  $ a_{10}^{*} $ , and  $ b_{10}^{*} $  values of 50.3, 0.2, and 1.3, respectively. The illuminating/viewing geometry was approximately  $ 0^{\circ}/45^{\circ} $  at a viewing distance about 25 cm. The tristimulus values were also calculated under the real light source and CIE 1964 standard colorimetric observer. To compare the difference between real light source and CIE illuminant D65, the relative colour differences between two sets of colour differences of 446 pairs were calculated. It was found that the average relative difference computed between a pair of samples was 0.17 CIELAB units. It implies the quality of the D65 simulator fitted in the viewing cabinet was quite satisfactory at least for the evaluation of colour differences. Finally, it was decided that the tristimulus values calculated from CIE illuminant D65 were used in the following data analysis.

A grey-scale method as arranged in Fig. 3 was used to scale the colour differences of sample pairs. The grey scale used in the experiment was prepared to have the same size and material as the samples being used. The scale was different from those typically used. It was specially designed to have a range from 1 to 5 with an interval of approximately 1 unit of  $ \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $ . The previous grey-scale differences were designed in a logarithmic scale stretched from 0 to 13.6, and each step is doubled in visual difference. $ ^{10} $ 

During the experiment, the “sample” pair is given in the bottom of the background (Fig. 3), and the five “grey-scale” pairs are presented in the top of the background. They are produced from the same printer with same substrate. A grey mask having the same colour as the background was used during the experiment. Hence, only one grey pair can be seen during the visual assessments.

Observers were asked to estimate by exploration of the visual grades for some large perceived colour difference pairs.

All observers were the students and staff at BIGC. They all passed the Ishihara vision test and had normal colour vision. Each pair was assessed by a panel of nine observers, and each observer repeated the trial three times, so that each colour pair was assessed 27 times. In total, 12,042 judgements were made.

Before the real experiment, observers were trained to assess colour difference using the grey-scale method. Before each observing session, observers were asked to adapt to the grey background field for about 1 min. The sequence of samples being assessed followed a random order for each observer.

They were instructed to conduct visual assessment using the five grey-scale pairs as references. If the colour difference of a sample pair was not equal to the colour difference of the closest grey scale, observers were encouraged to provide an intermediate step, e.g., 2.6 for a colour difference greater than grade 2 but smaller than grade 3. The pair having the largest average grade was 5.87, and the percentage of pairs with grade larger than 5.0 was 1.1% (i.e., five out of 446 pairs).

Figure 4 shows the line fitted between the grades of grey scale and their  $ \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $  values. All visual judgments in grades for each observer were transformed to visual colour difference ( $ \Delta V $ ) with the equation  $ \Delta V = 0.967 \times \text{grade} + 0.035 $ .

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

## Measure of Fit

Recently, the standardized residual sum of squares (STRESS) given in Eq. (1) has been introduced $ ^{11} $  as a measure between two sets of data such as the investigations of the intraobserver and interobserver variations and testing the performance of the colour-difference formulae.

<div style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://pplines-online.bj.bcebos.com/deploy/official/paddleocr/pp-ocr-vl//84748ad6-936a-4aab-bf89-5e73d0a6f821/markdown_3/imgs/img_in_chart_box_659_107_1077_517.jpg?authorization=bce-auth-v1%2FALTAKzReLNvew3ySINYJ0fuAMN%2F2025-12-30T05%3A11%3A45Z%2F-1%2F%2F90c85df8091799d98a7daa7c87e7adffaa177f847c6c3bd5c6f27af7c157a445" alt="Image" width="34%" /></div>


<div style="text-align: center;">FIG. 4. The linear regression between CIELAB colour difference and the grade of grey scale.</div>


 $$  STRESS=100\sqrt{\frac{\sum\left(\Delta V_{i}-f\Delta E_{i}\right)^{2}}{\sum\Delta V_{i}^{2}}} $$ 

where  $ f = \frac{\sum \Delta E_{i} \Delta V_{i}}{\sum \Delta E_{i}^{2}} $ 

In the following discussions, STRESS values are calculated assuming that the scaling factor f changes automatically according to the two datasets compared except in checking observer variations where f is set to one. For a perfect agreement between two datasets, STRESS will be zero. A higher STRESS value indicates a larger dispersion, i.e., a lower correlation between the two variables. A higher STRESS value indicates a poorer agreement between the two variables.

STRESS can also be used to test the statistical significance of difference between two formulae A and B. The testing hypothesis is described below, for which the residual error variance  $ V_{M} $  given in Eq. (2) was calculated.

1. Define the null and alternate hypotheses (two-tailed)

 $ H_{0} $ :  $ V_{A} = V_{B} $  (e.g., A and B without significant difference)

 $ H_{A} $ :  $ V_{A} \neq V_{B} $  (e.g., A and B with significant difference)

2. Calculate the F value as  $ F = V_{A}/V_{B} $  where

 $$ V_{\mathbf{M}}=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\Delta V_{i}-f_{\mathbf{M}}\Delta E_{\mathbf{M}i}\right)^{2}/(N-1)\qquad\mathbf{M}\in\{\mathbf{A},\mathbf{B}\} $$ 

or

 $$ F=\frac{V_{\mathrm{A}}}{V_{\mathrm{B}}}=\frac{\sum\left(\Delta V_{i}-f_{\mathrm{A}}\Delta E_{\mathrm{A}i}\right)^{2}}{\sum\left(\Delta V_{i}-f_{\mathrm{B}}\Delta E_{\mathrm{B}i}\right)^{2}}=\frac{STRESS_{\mathrm{A}}^{2}}{STRESS_{\mathrm{B}}^{2}} $$

The  $ f_{M} $  is calculated using f in Eq. (1), which is the slope between the visual results  $ \Delta V $  and the  $ \Delta E $  for models A and B, respectively. Finally, N is the number of samples in the dataset, and  $ df_{A} = df_{B} = N - 1 $  in this study. The results can be divided into five categories as shown below:

1. Model A is significantly better than model B when  $ F < F_{C} $ .

2. Model A is significantly worse than model B when  $ F > I/F_{C} $ .

3. Model A is insignificantly better than model B when  $ F_{C} \leq F < 1 $ .

4. Model A is insignificantly worse than model B when  $ 1 < F \leq 1/F_{C} $ .

5. Model A is equal to model B when F = 1.

## Observer Variations

Observer variations are usually divided into intraobserver and interobserver variability. Observer's intravariation is used to determine the variation of the visual assessments, or repeatability, of a particular observer. It was calculated between each observer's individual session and the mean of the three repeats. Finally, the three sets of visual colour-difference values were averaged. Observer intervariation represents the average deviation between individuals and the mean visual results for all observers. The intraobserver and interobserver variability in terms of STRESS for this study are shown in Table III.

The mean value of the intraobserver variability for the nine observers was 19.1 units ranging from 13.7 to 24.6, and the interobserver variability for all observers was 31.7 units ranging from 23.6 to 38.8. These results were in good agreement with those found by Cui and Luo $ ^{12} $  using Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) colours and by Guan and Luo $ ^{13} $  using surface colours, which all used grey-scale method. Note that the earlier studies used PF/3 measure, which is similar to STRESS in magnitude. Also, the f value in Eq. (1) to investigate observer variabilities was set to one for examining interobserver and intraobserver variability by measuring each individual against the mean observer data. Although it was reported that it could lead to unequal results when interchange between the two datasets compared to set f of one $ ^{14} $  i.e.,  $ \Delta E $  and  $ \Delta V $  in Eq. (1). However, the difference by exchanging  $ \Delta E $  and  $ \Delta V $  values is not large.

## Uniformities of Colour Spaces

The experimental data for each colour center were also used to fit an ellipsoid in CIELAB, CAM02-SCD, CAM02-UCS, CAM02-LCD, DIN99d, and OSA colour spaces, respectively. An ellipsoid equation in CIELAB space is given in Eq. (4):

 $$ \Delta E^{2}=\mathrm{g}_{11}\Delta a^{*2}+2g_{12}\Delta a^{*}\Delta b^{*}+g_{22}\Delta b^{*2}+g_{33}\Delta L^{*2} $$ 

where $g_{ij}$ are the ellipsoid coefficients, which were optimized to give the smallest STRESS value between the $\Delta E$ in Eq. (4) and $\Delta V$ values from the experimental data. When fitting the ellipsoid, the scaling factor $f$ in calculating STRESS was set to one to make the computed $\Delta E$ using Eq. (4) automatically to have the same scale as $\Delta V$.

Luo and Rigg $ ^{19} $  reported that the cross terms for  $ \Delta a^{*}-\Delta L^{*} $  and  $ \Delta b^{*}-\Delta L^{*} $  had insignificant contribution to the overall colour difference. In addition, as mentioned earlier, the current research was focused on chromatic differences with each pair having small contribution of lightness difference. These terms were excluded in Eq. (4). By removing the  $ g_{33} $  term in Eq. (4), an ellipsoid equation becomes an ellipse equation. Note that the sample pairs were prepared to have small  $ \Delta L^{*} $  differences. ## CONCLUSIONS

An experiment was conducted to investigate the performance of 10 colour-difference formulae and uniform colour spaces using grey-scale method. In total, 446 pairs of printed samples were prepared with an average  $ \Delta E_{ab,10}^{*} $  of 3 units. Each pair was totally assessed 27 times by a panel of nine observers with normal colour vision.

The results from this study are summarized below:

• Intraobserver variability is much lower than interobserver variability with STRESS values of 19 and 32, respectively, assuming f = 1 in Eq. (1).

• All formulae or spaces gave similar performance and performed accurately except CIELAB and OSA (larger than STRESS value of 32).

• The optimized formulae or spaces have no significant improvement against that the original formulae or space (i.e.  $ k_{L} = 1 $ ). Note that in industrial practice, many printing companies use  $ k_{L} = 2 $ . This should be corrected.

• By comparing different uniform colour spaces, it was found that OSA-Gp-Eu and DIN99d were close to the circle and had similar size, respectively. However, CAM02-UCS performed well for both measures.

• Comparing the present ellipses with those accumulated from the previous studies, it can be seen that the present ones filled well to the gaps of the previous ones. Also, they fit well to the general patterns in terms of ellipse orientation, shapes and sizes.

• Comparing the 11 ellipses obtained from the ZJU and the present data, a good agreement can be found between the two datasets.

## BOOK REVIEW

(Continued from page 390)

Relying on the matrix algebra introduced in the end of chapter 4, concepts like average, variance, and covariance are introduced as the elements of matrices and matrix operations. To my thinking, this introduces a level of complexity that may create more confusion than understanding. Trying to visualize a 31 dimensional space is pretty abstract, and the book’s use of a spectral distribution plot does not really help that concept.

The book shows a figure with 21 replicate readings of a cyan patch from pages taken from a press run and illustrates that the spectral curves are nearly indistinguishable on a plot whose abscissa ranges from 0 to 70 reflectance factor units. So, the author recommends plotting the difference between the average reading and each individual reading on a wavelength by wavelength basis where the abscissa now ranges from -1.0 to +1.0, with the individual readings mirrored about the 0 line. The author comments that some readers (aka color scientists and technologists) want to take the differences from the first acceptable sheet. But this, the book states, “… is not consistent with statistical thinking and results in more noisy data.” Such an attitude is a bit like Gunter Wyszecki’s anecdote about the duck hunter with the double-barreled shotgun, in which the barrels were misaligned. When the duck flew up, the hunter fired and shots passed to the right and to the left of the duck but statistically, on average, the duck was dead. There are times when “statistical thinking” does not describe economic reality and not testing the print run against the OK’d press sheet is one of those times.

It is here in Chapter 5 that the author introduces the concept of geometric intuition as an important aspect of understanding multivariate data in image analysis. At this point, the examples are scaled back to three or four dimensions, so that the plots can be shown correctly in the textbook. Here then, correlation among the variables is finally introduced and illustrated beautifully with a series of plots. These bivariate plots will be the foundation on which the textbook will introduce more advanced tools like principle components and cluster analysis in later chapters.

Chapter 6 generalizes from Chapter 3 and introduces multivariate statistical inferences including testing for Normality, Hotelling's  $ T^{2} $ , and Mahalonobis distance. There is an excellent discussion of the difference between a rectangular Bonferroni confidence region and the elliptical Hotelling confidence region.

Chapter 7 discusses principal components and root f for the variance-covariance matrix. The first part of the chapter shows satellite data with 152 spectral bands and then describes how to reduce the data by finding that 99.2% of the image variability can be reproduced with the first two principal components. Rules for stopping and for residual error analysis are also provided. There is enough detail that an imaging student, with a good stats package could duplicate the analysis on his/her own data.

Chapter 8 describes canonical correlation analysis. Chapter 9 covers Discrimination and Classification, which the author subtitles as Supervised Learning while Chapter 10 introduces Clustering, which the author subtitles as Unsupervised Learning. Both chapters show excellent examples from remote sensing images. There is a particularly clear description of the use of Minkowski metrics to replace Euclidean distances and how that affects the shape of the clusters. In addition, there are graphical examples of how the different clustering algorithms applied to the same image will result in different sets of clusters.

The three Appendices provide some rigor for the geometric intuition given in the chapters. Appendix A describes various probability distributions and their moments. It also includes, the now infrequently needed, ubiquitous tables of percentile points for the standard Normal, Chi-Squared, Student's t and F distributions. Appendix B contains descriptions of the various datasets and a reference to where the data can be found on the Internet. Appendix C is titled, Miscellanea and contains various mathematical relationships and a list of the symbols and abbreviations used in the textbook.

Aside from the occasional jab at the imaging scientist or graphic engineer, the book really is a useful primer. It does not cover any topic in enough depth to make the reader fully competent, but it will provide just enough insight for one to know what to look for or what to read to get that depth. I think that this book will find a place on my desk.

Danny C. Rich
