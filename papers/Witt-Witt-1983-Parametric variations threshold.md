# Parametric Variations in a Threshold Color-Difference Ellipsoid for Green Painted Samples $ ^{*} $ 

Following the CIE guidelines for coordinated research on color-difference evaluation, a set of painted color samples was produced near the green reference color. Visual experiments were carried out by two different methods, absolute threshold and pair comparison. Perceptibility ellipsoids at and near threshold were determined for different mathematical models and for different groupings of observers. The main findings are: insensitivity to the method of optimization, inhomogeneity of the total set of answers but homogeneity within ranked observer groups, and change of volume but similarity of shape of the ellipsoids for extreme groups. Comparisons with other visual studies are discussed.

## I ntroduction

The Color Difference Subcommittee of CIE Technical Committee 1.3 (Colorimetry) has provided guidelines for coordinated research on color-difference evaluation. $ ^{1} $  Their main proposals were to concentrate further experimental work on the perceptibility of color differences using samples within close proximity of five different colors (step 1) and to find effects of parameter variations on color-difference ellipsoids in tristimulus space (step 2) before entering into a complete mapping of color space (step 3) and development of a new color-difference formula (step 4). Following the lines of step 1 and step 2 we began with a project of developing threshold ellipsoids for color-difference perception of glossy painted samples. In the preliminary report $ ^{2} $  we presented first results of evaluating threshold ellipsoids near to the green reference color. In this article these results are presented in more detail, asking about variations of the shape of the threshold ellipsoid if the mathematical model of optimization, the grouping of observers, or the method of color-difference estimation is changed.

## Materials

Glossy painted samples were produced using a two-component acrylic lacquer which has high lightfastness. The lacquer was coated on paperboard with a doctor blade using a film thickness sufficient for complete hiding. After drying the paperboard was glued to sheet aluminum in order to maintain a plane surface. The colors of the samples were chosen to be arranged randomly in tristimulus space near the green reference color:  $ x_{10} = 0.248 $ ,  $ y_{10} = 0.362 $ ,  $ Y_{10} = 24.0 $  (illuminant  $ D_{65} $ ). A selection of 54 more-or-less randomly distributed color samples were produced by application of color recipe formulation without changing pigmentation, but only concentration, in order to reduce metamerism. Color measurements were done with a Zeiss DMC25 spectrophotometer equipped with  $ 45^{\circ}/0^{\circ} $  measuring geometry, and tristimulus values were calculated for the CIE 1964 supplementary standard colorimetric observer and standard illuminant  $ D_{65} $ . The average tristimulus values of the “color cloud” in tristimulus space (Fig. 1) and its standard deviations were  $ x_{10} = 0.2514 \pm 0.0010 $ ,  $ y_{10} = 0.3638 \pm 0.0011 $ ,  $ Y_{10} = 24.30 \pm 0.45 $ . Paired combinations of color samples allowed for presentation of color differences from 0 to  $ 5 \Delta E_{ab}^{*} $  units. Color measurements were done for all samples before and after each visual test series. Slight variations of tristimulus values did not significantly influence their differences.

The pair combinations selected are indicated in Fig. 1. No common reference sample was used. During all four sessions an operator presented the sample pairs and recorded the answers. The first sequence was randomized by random numbers that were kept constant for all observers. All exchanges were done by rotating the sample pairs  $ 180^{\circ} $  around the direction of viewing so that the same area of the color pair always appeared within the viewing field.

Before starting the first session, all observers were shown a color pair with zero color difference and a pair with just clearly visible color difference to check their ability to discern both situations.

For the second series the question was:

II. Is the color difference of the sample pair presented larger than that of the standard pair placed close to it?

The experiment of series II was carried out about one year after that of series I. The sample pairs were in part the same as in series I (30), and in part new ones which were added mainly in the region of larger color differences (up to  $ \Delta E_{ab}^{*} = 1.9 $ ). The operator was instructed to exchange left and right positions of sample pairs and of the reference and test pair regularly between sessions so that each possible arrangement occurred once.

## Mathematical Models

A color-difference ellipsoid is defined in the usual way in $\Delta x_{10}, \Delta y_{10}, \Delta Y_{10}$ space¹ by

 $$ \begin{array}{r}{\Delta E^{2}=g_{11}(\Delta x_{10})^{2}+g_{22}(\Delta y_{10})^{2}+g_{33}(\Delta Y_{10})^{2}}\\ {+2g_{12}\Delta x_{10}\Delta y_{10}+2g_{13}\Delta x_{10}\Delta Y_{10}}\\ {+2g_{23}\Delta y_{10}\Delta Y_{10}.}\end{array} $$ 

This equation can be used to calculate probability functions for positive answers. The mathematical task is to optimize the unknown parameters of a probability function for best correspondence between observed and calculated probabilities. This was done for different functions and different methods of optimization as follows:

 $$ P_{i}=(1+e^{\alpha-\beta\Delta E_{i}})^{-1}. $$ 

This is the logistic function proposed by the CIE,$^{1}$ in which $i$ is the number of the pair and $\alpha,\beta$ are parameters.

 $$ P_{i}=(1-p)e^{-(\Delta E_{i})^{2}/2}. $$ 

This is the Gaussian density function including a false-alarm probability $p$ as used by Rich et al.$^{4}$

(c) Optimization according to the maximum-likelihood method $ ^{1,5} $ :

 $$ \operatorname*{m a x}\sum_{i=1}^{N}[r_{i}\ln P_{i}+(n-r_{i})\ln{(1-P_{i})}], $$ 

where  $ r_{i} $  is the number of positive answers, n is the number of presentations of pairs, and N the number of pairs.

(d) Minimization of quadratic deviation, used by Strocka et al. $ ^{6} $ 

 $$ \operatorname*{m i n}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(P_{i}-\overline{{P}}_{i})^{2}, $$ 

where  $ \overline{P}_{i} $  is the expected probability of positive answers.

(e) Replacement of the linear difference $\Delta Y_{10}$ by the difference in the logarithm $\Delta \log Y_{10}$, which has been used by Brown and MacAdam.$^{7}$

For optimization of the unknown parameters of the probability functions, a program PAROPT was used which minimizes a function by an iterative algorithm without using differential derivatives. $ ^{8} $ 

## V ariations Due to Mathematical Formalism

The first task was to find a mathematical procedure that serves best for adapting parameter values to the experimental situation. The following cases were studied: case 1, combination of (a) with (c); case 2, combination of (a) with (d); case 3, combination of (b) with (c); and case 4, same as case 3 plus (e). As a measure of the quality of the optimized function the standard deviations  $ s_{P} $  between observed and calculated probabilities of sample pairs were estimated. The resulting values were case 1,  $ s_{P}=0.0733 $ ; case 2,  $ s_{P}=0.0731 $ ; case 3,  $ s_{P}=0.0757 $ ; and case 4,  $ s_{P}=0.0753 $ . Looking at the corresponding ellipsoids, one finds only slight variations within the chromaticity plane and a compression along the  $ \Delta Y_{10} $  axis for case 4, which favors  $ \Delta \log Y_{10} $  over  $ \Delta Y_{10} $  only because of a better scale factor by chance (same order of magnitude as in directions  $ \Delta x_{10} $  and  $ \Delta y_{10} $ ). The variations seem to be small enough to allow for an arbitrary choice. We shall follow the proposal of the CIE guideline $ ^{1} $  and use case 1 henceforth.

On applying a Friedman test for checking the homogeneity of the answers of all observers, a high significance for inhomogeneity was found  $ (P < 0.001) $ . This test, on the other hand, gives rank numbers to the observers that can be interpreted as a ranking of the severity of their judgments; these are listed in Table II. By forming smaller groups of observers with similar rank numbers, homogeneity within groups is found  $ (P > 0.05) $ .

The Spearman rank correlation coefficient between observers provides an average value and standard deviation for all 300 observer pairs possible:  $ \overline{\rho}=0.49\pm0.13 $ . Most of the individual coefficients are highly significantly different from zero (P<0.001), which means that there are almost no uncorrelated answers.

These statistical results indicate that one should be careful with the interpretation of averages and that group splitting may be a useful procedure when one is seeking parametric effects.

## Discussion

The experiments by Rich, Billmeyer, and Howe $ ^{5} $  on paint samples have been cited in the CIE guidelines $ ^{1} $  as an example of color-difference studies of surface colors. Meanwhile Strocka, Brockes, and Paffhausen $ ^{6} $  published results on red textile samples following the proposal of the CIE guidelines. They indicated some problems with the mathematical optimization procedure. As is shown here, the method of optimizing a function to the experimental data does not seem to be crucial, but it must be mentioned that the iteration procedure was started several times with the parameters obtained previously as new starting values to check their validity. On the other hand, Rich and Billmeyer $ ^{9} $  also reported convergence problems when using the maximum-likelihood method.

The scattering of the experimental data around the optimized function is similar to that found in other studies. $ ^{5,6} $  The expectation that scattering might be reduced for small color differences in a pair-comparison method was not fulfilled. Possibly the size of the color difference of the

standard pair is critical in a forced-choice procedure. If it is near to threshold, more uncertain answers for identical test pairs will result. Strocka et al. $ ^{6} $  found a well-defined approach to  $ \Delta E = 0 $  when using a larger standard color difference of  $ \Delta E_{ab}^{*} = 2 $ , while in our experiments it was 0.8. Another result of their work was not checked by us, namely whether the direction of the color difference of the standard pair played a significant role in regard to the shape of the color-difference ellipsoid. But indirectly the answer is given by the similarity of the probability functions or ellipsoids for the absolute-threshold and pair-comparison methods, which says there is no significant influence.

The evaluation of color-difference ellipses at constant lightness $ ^{10} $  and full ellipsoids $ ^{7,11,12} $  based on observations made with visual colorimeters showed a strong effect of the observer on their shapes. In this work no data analysis of single observers can be given because the inherent variances of a single observer are too large due to the method used. Grouping the observers introduces an averaging effect and reduces the individuality, but if such groups are homogeneous no statistical argument prohibits the interpretation of the group data. Unexpectedly, a stabilization of group averages has been found here. The correlation between severe- and lenient-judging groups is high, the corresponding ellipsoids being very similar in shape and differing mainly in volume. A second finding is that the average probability function is largely unaffected by the experimental technique used as is clearly shown by comparing Fig. 4 with Fig. 6.

The shapes of our ellipsoids are not easily compared with those of other workers because, on the one hand, no one has used colors near the vicinity of the color region used here, and on the other hand, the experimental parameters are mostly different. $ ^{5,7,10-12} $  A tentative comparison of nearby color-difference ellipses in the chromaticity plane $ ^{7,10-12} $ 

shows the  $ g_{ik} $  coefficients to be of the same order of magnitude. A closer look at the study of Brown $ ^{11} $  shows his nearest color centers 20 and 21 to have slightly larger semiaxes, the orientation of the major axis being most similar for color center 21 ( $ \theta = 154^{\circ} $ ). In the work of Wyszecki and Fielder, $ ^{12} $  the nearest color center is 26. Weighting their ellipse parameters by a factor of  $ 1/\sqrt{7.81} $ , as Robertson $ ^{13} $  did because of their statistical meaning, results in very similar semiaxes. Only the orientations are different, the nearest one being that of observer GW ( $ \theta = 103^{\circ} $ ). In the acceptability data of Kuehni $ ^{14} $  his ellipse ME seems to have similar orientation ( $ \theta \simeq 112^{\circ} $ ).

Boynton and Kambe $ ^{15,16} $  have tried to measure chromatic difference steps of moderate size along critical axes (the direction toward the tritanopic copunctal point and the direction toward the deuteranopic fusion point at x = 1.0, y = 0.0). Hypothetically, they believe in linear summation rules by which the major axis of a color-difference figure can be constructed within a parallelogram between the critical axes. Such a construction can be tried if these critical axes are taken to be the same for the  $ 10^{\circ} $  as for the  $ 2^{\circ} $  observer. The major axis of such a figure ( $ \theta = 119^{\circ} $ ) is only slightly rotated in comparison with the major axes of the corresponding average ellipses ( $ \theta = 131^{\circ} $  and  $ 124^{\circ} $ ). It may be questioned whether the Boynton–Kambe model is correct for small color differences near to threshold, because of discrepancies found between the perception of small and larger color differences. $ ^{13} $  Nevertheless, the near coincidence of the corresponding angles may not be accidental.
