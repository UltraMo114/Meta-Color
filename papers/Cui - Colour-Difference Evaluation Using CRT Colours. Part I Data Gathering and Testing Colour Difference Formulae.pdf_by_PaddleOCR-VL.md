# Colour-Difference Evaluation Using CRT Colours. Part I: Data Gathering and Testing Colour Difference Formulae

Guihua Cui, $ ^{1} $  M. Ronnier Luo, $ ^{1} $  * B. Rigg, $ ^{1} $  Wei Li $ ^{2} $ 

 $ ^{1} $  Colour & Imaging Institute, University of Derby, UK

 $ ^{2} $  Beijing Institute of Technology, Beijing, China

Received 22 July 2000; accepted 1 September 2000

Abstract: A large set of colour discrimination data based upon Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) colours was accumulated. The experiment was carried out to study the effect of changes in viewing parameters on perceived colour differences. It was divided into sixteen phases. Each phase was conducted under a different set of experimental conditions including different sample sizes, backgrounds, frames, widths, and colours of separation between pairs of samples. For each phase, 134 pairs of colours with average 2.7 CIELAB colour difference were studied. The colours closely corresponded to the five colour centers proposed by the CIE. Each pair was assessed by 10 or 20 observers using the gray scale method. The visual results from the present study were also used to test six colour-difference formulae: CIELAB, CMC, BFD, CIE94, LCD, and CIEDE2000. The latter outperformed the other formulae and is the new CIE colour-difference equation. The current results were also compared with the other datasets based upon surface colours. The present results are consistent with those obtained for surface colours. © 2001 John Wiley & Sons, Inc. Col Res Appl, 26, 394–402, 2001

Key words: color difference metrics; viewing parameters; CIELAB; CMC; BFD; CIE94; CIEDE2000

## I NTRODUCTION

The perceived colour difference of a pair of samples can change according to different viewing parameters. This is known as the parametric effect. Typical parameters that may affect visual colour differences are different sample separations, background colours, illuminants, luminance levels, the physical sizes, the magnitudes of colour differences, textures, and modes of colours (surface or self-luminous). The CIE Technical Committee 1-28 on Parameters Affecting Colour-difference Evaluation $ ^{1} $  was formed to coordinate researchers conducting research in this area. Most of the colour discrimination datasets for surface colours were accumulated under a set of similar reference viewing conditions using paint or textile samples. Using surface colours, it is tedious and difficult to accurately prepare the desired samples. In the present study, the CRT colours were used. The desired colours and colour differences can be obtained much more easily and accurately. This is the first part of a two-part article, which describes the techniques involved for data gathering, testing the performances of various colour difference equations, and comparing the present CRT data with those based upon surface samples. The second part of the article focuses on the impact of different viewing parameters affecting the colour-difference evaluation.

## EXPERIMENTAL

All colour stimuli in this study were displayed on a CRT display. They were divided into three groups. The first group includes 85 pairs (17 pairs for each of the five colour centers studied). The five colour centers were chosen to be close to the CIE gray, red, yellow, green, and blue colour centers,² which have been comprehensively studied by a number of research groups.³-⁸ The CIELAB values for the colour centers used in this study are given in Table II. The colour coordinates of the CRT stimuli were selected from sets prepared by Cheung and Rigg³ using wool material. These sample pairs were carefully chosen to give a good coverage of all directions from the colour center in the x,y chromaticity diagram. The sample-pair distribution for the gray CIE center plotted in the ΔxΔy plane is given in Fig. 1. There are twelve pairs located in the ΔxΔy planes, two pairs in each of the ΔxΔY and ΔyΔY planes, and one pair in the ΔY direction.

The second group consists of 10 lightness difference pairs (two additional pairs for each of the five centers). The third group consists of 39 neutral pairs demonstrating lightness differences with an interval of approximately  $ \Delta L^{*} = 2.5 $  from  $ L^{*} = 0-100 $ . The average CIELAB colour difference for all sample pairs studied was 2.7. The distribution of the  $ \Delta E $  values is shown in Fig. 2.

## CRT Characterization 20 inch

A SAMSUNG SyncMaster 20Gls CRT display was used in the study. It was driven by a 24-bit display card and ran under  $ 800 \times 600 $  resolution. Before carrying out the characterization, the brightness and contrast controls of the CRT display together with the gain and off-set controls for each of the red, green, and blue channels were adjusted. The colour temperature of the white point of the CRT was set to 6500 K with luminance of  $ 80 \pm 2 \, cd/m^{2} $ . The characterization was conducted only once before the experiment during the whole experimental period (about 2 years).

In the present study, the CRT was characterized using the GOG model developed by Berns et al. $ ^{9-11} $  Computer-controlled CRT displays are described by a two-stage model.

## Colour Measuring Instrument and CRT Performance

A Photo Research PR1980BX spectroradiometer was used for colour measurement. Various studies were conducted to study the instrument and CRT performance. The first study was carried out to investigate the medium-term stability of the CRT over a day. The colour coordinates of the five CIE colour centers given in Table II were first converted to RGB DAC values using Eq. (1). The CRT colours were repeatedly measured over a period of 4 hours. This resulted in 40 readings for each colour. The MCDM (the mean colour difference calculated from a set of mean colour differences) was used to indicate the performance of colour variation in terms of CIELAB  $ \Delta E $ . For each of the five colours measured, the mean CIELAB values were calculated. These were used to calculate  $ 40 \Delta E $  values between the mean and each individual reading. The mean for each colour center and all colours are given in Table III.

The results show an overall MCDM of 0.12. This indicates that the CRT used in this study was very stable at least over a 4-h period. Note that during the experimental period, the experimenter adjusted brightness and contrast controls only after the CRT had warmed up for 30 min at the beginning of each working day to ensure that the monitor white corresponding to a luminance value of  $ 80 \, cd/m^2 $ . Hence, this performance (MCDM = 0.12) is very satisfactory.

## V isual Assessments

Each pair was assessed more than 20 times by a panel of 10–20 observers using the gray scale psychophysical method. All observers were undergraduate students from the Beijing Institute of Technology and had normal colour vision according to the Ishihara test. They had little experience in scaling colour differences.

The gray scale method used for visual assessment was identical to that used by Luo and Rigg, $ ^{12} $  Kuo and Luo, $ ^{13,14} $  and Guan and Luo. $ ^{8} $  The experimental condition is illustrated in Fig. 3. The gray scale used in the present study consisted of five gray samples (grades) numbered 1–5 and a standard the same as the Grade '5' sample in Fig. 3. The grades were similar to the standard gray scales used in fastness testing for assessing colour change in the textile industry.  $$ PF=100(\gamma+V_{AB}+CV/100-r), $$ 

where r represents the correlation coefficient, CV and  $ \gamma $ , proposed by Coates et al. $ ^{15} $ , and  $ V_{AB} $  derived by Schultz, $ ^{16} $  respectively.

However, previous studies found that in some cases the correlation coefficient  $ (r) $  was quite inconsistent with the other measures, and it was thus decided to use only the remaining three measures. $ ^{8,17,18} $  The PF/3 value is calculated using Eq. (4):

 $$ PF/3=100(\gamma-1+V_{AB}+CV/100)/3. $$ 

Note that it is desirable to consider errors in percentage terms in this type of work. If one colour-difference formula is identical to a second, except that it gives  $ \Delta E $  values twice as big, the absolute error in  $ \Delta E $  doubles, but the percentage error remains the same, consistent with the fact that the equations are of equal merit.

For experiments conducted using the gray scale method, the raw data in Grade  $ (G) $  units were transformed to the visual difference for each pair,  $ \Delta V $ , using Eq. (5):

The coefficients in Eq. (5) were obtained by fitting a third-order polynomial equation between the  $ \Delta E $  and Grade values in Table IV. (5) and the arithmetic mean of the  $ \Delta V $  values for each pair was calculated. The mean values were used to test various colour difference formulae and to reveal the parametric effects.

## TESTING THE PERFORMANCE OF COLOUR-DIFFERENCE FORMULAE

More recent advanced colour-difference formulae such as BFD, $ ^{19,20} $  CMC, $ ^{21} $  CIE94, $ ^{22} $  LCD, $ ^{23} $  and CIEDE2000 $ ^{24} $  are modifications from the CIELAB. $ ^{25} $  A generic equation given in Eq. (6) is used to represent these formulae. The latter is the new CIE colour-difference formula.

 $$ \Delta E=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta L^{*}}{k_{L}S_{L}}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\Delta C^{*}}{k_{C}S_{C}}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\Delta H^{*}}{k_{H}S_{H}}\right)^{2}+R_{T}f(\Delta C^{*},\Delta H^{*})}. $$ 

In the normal form of the formulae, the  $ k_{L} $ ,  $ k_{C} $ , and  $ k_{H} $  values in Eq. (6) are equal to one, but, as suggested by the CIE TC1-28, $ ^{1} $  optimized values may indicate the impact of different viewing parameters. The  $ R_{T} $  value is zero, except for the BFD, LCD, and CIEDE2000 formulae. For CIELAB, the values of  $ S_{L} $ ,  $ S_{C} $ , and  $ S_{H} $  are all equal to one. The  $ S_{L} $  value is equal to one for CIE94.

The performances of the six colour-difference formulae were tested using the present dataset. Each formula was first tested using the visual results from each of the 16 phases separately in terms of PF/3 measure. The PF/3 values were then averaged to represent the performance of each formula and are given in Table VI under titles of ‘ $ k_{L} = 1 $ ' and 'Optimized  $ k_{L} $ ', respectively. ## COMPARISON WITH OTHER STUDIES

The average results for each colour center from the 16 phases were used to compare with four earlier datasets produced by Cheung and Rigg, $ ^{3} $  RIT–DuPont, $ ^{4} $  Witt (1999), $ ^{7} $  and Witt (1983,1987). $ ^{5,6} $  All four of these datasets were generated using surface colours corresponding to the five CIE colour centers. (Witt published two articles in 1983 and 1987. The former included two CIE green centers and the latter included four centers. Hence, there were six centers including two green centers.) Chromatic ellipses and ellipsoids were calculated in CIELAB  $ a^{*}b^{*} $  plane. The ellipsoid equation with 4 coefficients (Eq. 7) was used to fit experimental results for each colour center:

 $$ \Delta E^{2}=k_{1}\Delta L^{*2}+k_{2}\Delta a^{*2}+k_{3}\Delta b^{*2}+k_{4}\Delta a^{*}\Delta b^{*}. $$ 

The other two possible terms  $ \Delta a^{*}\Delta L^{*} $  and  $ \Delta b^{*}\Delta L^{*} $  were not used, because it was found in Luo and Rigg's study $ ^{12} $  that these terms have very small effect on the total  $ \Delta E $ . Thus  $ k_{1} $  to  $ k_{4} $  were optimized to give the best fit for each colour center. Setting  $ \Delta E $  to a constant value allows  $ \Delta L^{*} $ ,  $ \Delta a^{*} $ , and  $ \Delta b^{*} $  values corresponding to an ellipsoid to be calculated for each colour center. Setting  $ \Delta L^{*} $  to zero allows the corresponding ellipse to be calculated.

For the present dataset, ellipse and ellipsoid equations were fitted to each of the 16 phases. The ellipse or ellipsoid parameters were then averaged to represent the present dataset.

The visual scales for each study are different. The sizes of all five ellipses in each dataset were adjusted by a single scaling factor  $ [0.93, 0.92, 0.51, \text{and } 0.98 \text{ for the present study, RIT–DuPont, Witt (1999), and Witt (1983–1987), respectively}] $ , to bring the results on to the same scale as that of Cheung and Rigg. $ ^{3} $  All the adjusted ellipses from each source are plotted in the CIELAB  $ a^{*}b^{*} $  diagram in Fig. 6. It is clear that the ellipses in each center agree well. The worst agreement can be found for the yellow and blue centers.

A quantitative comparison was carried out (between the present and each of the earlier sets of results) using the method developed by Strocka et al. $ ^{26} $  by comparing the predicted  $ \Delta E $  values between two ellipse or ellipsoid equations using 500 randomly generated colour differences. The results are given in Table IX and X, respectively. Compared to the present data, the mean variations using the ellipse equation were 16, 10, 18, and 17 PF/3 values for the Cheung and Rigg, RIT–DuPont, Witt (1999), and Witt (1983–1987) datasets, respectively. The agreement is generally very good, and that with the RIT–DuPont results is particularly good. By far the worst agreements were those for the Witt results for the yellow center, consistent with the qualitative impression gained from Fig. 6. Note that, although there is an appreciable difference in orientation of the blue ellipses, they are all consistent in not pointing towards the neutral point. ## CONCLUSIONS

The current experiment was conducted to study parametric effects using CRT colours. It was divided into 16 phases. For each phase, 134 pairs of colours were assessed about 20 times by a panel of 10 or 20 observers using the gray scale psychophysical method. The conclusions are summarized below.

Six colour-difference formulae were tested using the current experimental results. The five newer colour-difference formulae (CIEDE2000, BFD, LCD, CMC, and CIE94) except CMC outperformed the CIELAB formula. The CIEDE2000 colour-difference formula outperformed the other formulae, in particular for the blue and neutral areas. It was developed to fit the best available datasets based upon surface colours and also gave the best fit to the present dataset. This implies that there is a great similarity between the results obtained from the CRT and surface media. Also, the CIEDE2000 equation gives the most accurate predictions to all available datasets, and thus was recommended as the new CIE colour-difference formula.

The present results were also compared with the earlier datasets for surface colours corresponding to the five CIE colour centers: Cheung and Rigg, RIT–DuPont, Witt (1983–1987), and Witt (1999) in terms of colour discrimination ellipses and ellipsoids. In general, all the five datasets agree with each other very well, with an average variation of about 16%. This strongly suggests that the difference between the CRT and surface datasets is small.
