## Abstract

The paper describes a comprehensive test to evaluate the performance of current colour-difference models using available experimental datasets. In total, 28 individual datasets were accumulated to test 17 colour-difference formulae, 13 of them based on Uniform Colour Spaces (UCSs) in terms of the Standardized Residual Sum of Squares (STRESS) measure. The 28 datasets were divided into three groups: Large Colour-Difference data (LCD), Small Colour-Difference data for surface colours (SCDs), and Small Colour Difference data for display colours (SCDd). For each colour model, four versions were tested: the original model, and that including  $ k_{L} $ -Gamma- and  $ k_{L} $ /Gamma, which are the lightness parametric factor, the colour-difference exponent factor, and the combination of both, respectively, optimized to fit particular dataset(s). The statistical F-test was applied to test the difference between each pair of models. Furthermore, parametric effects between the large/small colour-difference magnitudes, and between surface/display colours were investigated. The results showed that CAM16-UCS significantly outperformed the other models for all groups. It accurately predicted all types of data and should be proposed for colour-difference evaluation across all industries.

Colour difference has been extensively investigated for over a century and, as a result, a number of colour-difference formulae and uniform colour spaces have been developed. The formula is recommended by the CIE to visually assess colour difference under a set of defined reference viewing conditions $ ^{10} $  given in Table 1. Note that the set of conditions were mainly provided for the surface colour industries. However, CIEDE2000 does not have an associated colour space. So, it cannot be used for many applications requiring a Uniform Colour Space (UCS), for example, for gamut volume calculation, gamut mapping, image compression, imaging enhancement for the imaging industries, colour quality for the lighting industry, colour constancy, metamerism, fastness testing for the surface colour industries. The current CIE recommended approximately UCS is still CIELAB, for which abundant evidence has shown that it has poor performance in predicting small colour differences  $ (\Delta E^{*}_{\mathrm{ab}} < 5) $ . Hence, an improved UCS has long been desired by industry.

With this in mind, the CIE TC 1–55 was formed in 1999 with a goal to propose a uniform color space for industrial colour-difference evaluation. However, their mission was not accomplished as reported in 2016, $ ^{11} $  i.e. although the three UCSs (CAM02-UCS, $ ^{12} $  DIN99d, $ ^{13} $  OSA-GP-Eu $ ^{14} $ ) outperformed the other UCSs investigated, and gave similar performance for the visual datasets available in 2001, they did not perform statistically significantly better than CIEDE2000. This was the expected outcome because all the UCSs were mainly derived by fitting the four datasets which were combined to optimize CIEDE2000. $ ^{4,7,15,16} $  Note CAM02-UCS was derived also including large colour-difference datasets (see later). Thus, the way forward is to conduct a comprehensive test using new datasets to cover more applications that exceed the reference conditions specified in Table 1.

In 2006, CAM02-UCS, $ ^{12} $  based on the CIE 2002 colour appearance model, CIECAM02, $ ^{17} $  was derived to be able to predict colour differences under different viewing conditions. More recently, CIECAM16, $ ^{18} $  has been recommended to supersede CIECAM02 $ ^{17} $  to overcome some mathematical drawbacks of that model. However, CAM16-UCS, the UCS derived as an extension to CAM16, $ ^{30} $  was not accepted as a CIE recommendation. The reason was that the matrix used to convert from XYZ tristimulus values to RGB cone responses was developed from 2° colour matching data, whereas recent colour-difference data were based on a 10° field of view. To clarify this, Li et al. $ ^{19} $  performed a computer simulation to generate reflectance data corresponding to the XYZ data in the four colour-difference datasets. $ ^{1-4} $  They concluded that the choice of 2 or 10° colour-matching functions (CMFs), had no statistical impact on the prediction of the visual difference derived with a 10° visual field using different colour-difference formulae. The colour differences calculated from all the models tested differed by less than 2 STRESS units. In addition, Xu et al. $ ^{20} $  conducted a visual experiment using colour-difference pairs that were judged using 2, 4, 10 and 20° fields of view and found that there was little difference between the colour differences calculated with the 2 and 10° CMFs, and the effect was small compared with the inter- and intra-observer variability, i.e. the effect was less than 10% in terms of STRESS units.

Table 3 introduces the 17 colour-difference models tested. For each model, the authors, year, a description of the model (such as perceptual quantizer [PQ], power, logarithmic, hyperbolic, etc.) and the datasets in Table 1 used to fit each model, are listed. Note that it was found that CAM02-UCS gave the same performance in terms of STRESS value as CAM16-UCS and so only CAM16-UCS was tested in the current study.

The generic structure of each model is given by Equation (1).

 $$ \Delta E=\left(\sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta L}{k_{L}}\right)^{2}+\Delta C^{2}+\Delta H^{2}}\right)^{Gamma} $$ 

where  $ \Delta L $ ,  $ \Delta C $ ,  $ \Delta H $  are the lightness, chroma and hue differences, respectively;  $ k_{L} $  and Gamma are the parameters, which are used to adjust the value of colour difference to accommodate a particular set of viewing conditions, Table 1, such as textile/coating, surface/display, LCD/SCD.

To test each colour model, four versions are considered: V1: the original version by setting  $ k_{L}=1 $  and Gamma = 1, V2: the  $ k_{L} $ -version by optimizing only the value of  $ k_{L} $  in Equation (1), V3: the Gamma version by optimizing only the value of Gamma in Equation (1), V4: the  $ k_{L}/Gamma $  version, by optimizing both the value of  $ k_{L} $  and the value Gamma simultaneously. The optimization was carried out to minimize the STRESS value (Equation [2]), as recommended by TC 1–55. $ ^{11} $ 

The most frequently used metric, the Standardized Residual Sum of Squares (STRESS) $ ^{11} $  (see Equation [2]), was used to evaluate the disagreement between the values of colour-difference predicted by the models ( $ \Delta E $ ) and the visual results ( $ \Delta V $ ).

 $$ STRESS=100\sqrt{\frac{\sum\left(f\Delta E_{i}-\Delta V_{i}\right)^{2}}{\sum\Delta V_{i}^{2}}}, $$ 

 $$  where f=\frac{\sum\Delta E_{i}\Delta V_{i}}{\sum\Delta E_{i}^{2}}, $$ 

where f is a scaling factor used to adjust the values of the measured colour-difference,  $ \Delta E $ , such that they fall on the same scale as the values of visual colour-difference,  $ \Delta V $ . A STRESS value of zero indicates a perfect agreement between two sets of data. It expresses the performance of the colour model as percentage error of prediction.

The performance between any two models was statistically tested using the F-test:

 $$ F=\frac{STRESS_{A}^{2}}{STRESS_{B}^{2}} $$ 

where  $ STRESS_{A} $  is the STRESS value calculated for model A and  $ STRESS_{B} $  is the STRESS value calculated for model B.

Then, designating as  $ F_{c} $  the critical value of the two-tailed F-distribution for a given confidence level (usually 95%) and (N-1, N-1) degrees of freedom, it can be concluded that the colour-difference model A is:

- significantly better than model B when $F < F_{c}$ (score 10)

- insignificantly better than model B when  $ F_{c} \leq F < 1 $  (score 7.5)

- equal to model B when F = 1 (score 5).

- insignificantly worse than model B when  $ 1 < F \leq 1/ $ 

 $ F_{c} $  (score 2.5)

- significantly worse than model B when  $ F > 1/F_{c} $  (score 0).

Note the score shown in the bracket for each above condition was added to easy comparison between different models' performances.

## 3 | THE PERFORMANCE OF THE ORIGINAL,  $ K_{L} $ , GAMMA AND  $ K_{L}/ $  GAMMA VERSIONS OF EACH COLOUR MODEL

As said before there are four versions for each colour model according to the values of the  $ k_{L} $  and Gamma parameters. The three improved versions of each model (named  $ k_{L} $ , Gamma,  $ k_{L}/Gamma $ ) were developed by optimizing the parameters to achieve minimum STRESS value between the Equation (1) predictions and visual results for each individual dataset in Table 2. For each group (i.e. SCDs, SCDd and LCD), the STRESS values for each version for all the datasets were averaged. The results found are listed in Table 4 for each group.

Table 4 main results can be summarized as follows:

• All models predicted the LCD data more accurately than the SCDs data by approximately 30%. This was caused by observers being able to judge larger colour differences less variation than smaller colour differences. $ ^{26} $ 

• Comparing the performance of the models in the SCDs group, CAM16-UCS ranked first, followed by CIEDE2000 and ULAB. As noted earlier, these three models were fitted to the same datasets. $ ^{1-4} $ 

• Comparing the performance of the models in the SCDd group, CAM16-UCS ranked first, followed by CIE94.

• Finally, for the LCD group, CAM16-UCS performed the best, followed by ULAB.

- From above, the overall result seems quite conclusive, i.e. CAM16-UCS performs the best for the datasets in all three groups.

The F-test was conducted as shown in Table 5-7 for the SCDs, SCDd, LCD groups respectively. The orange (grey) cells indicate that the model in the column is significantly better (worse) than the model in the row. This means that in columns of these Tables a good model should have many orange cells. A white cell means that there is no significant difference between the two models.

Comparing the results of the F-test on the models in the SCDs group (Table 5), CAM16-UCS performed the best, slightly better than CIEDE2000 and ULAB, and it outperformed all the other models. Comparing the results of the F-test on the models in the SCDd group (Table 6), CAM16-UCS and CIE94 significantly outperformed the other models. Finally, comparing the results of the F-test on the models in the LCD group (Table 7), CAM16-UCS and  $ J_{z}a_{z}b_{z} $  performed the best. Overall, the results from Tables 4 and 5-7 lead to the same conclusion, i.e., CAM16-UCS gives the overall best performance across all three groups of datasets.

Figure 1 is an alternative way to visualize the results shown in Table 5-7. As we can see CAM16-UCS achieved the best results (with very high scores) for SCDs, SCDd and LCD groups. Surprisingly the CIELUV formula, frequently used by the lighting industry, achieved a score of 0 for the SCDd group (see Table 6).

Note when considering each individual dataset rather than the combined data group, the difference between different versions for some datasets was statistically insignificant.

Table 9 lists the mean values of  $ k_{L} $  and Gamma parameters for each colour model and each one of the three groups. When comparing the  $ k_{L} $  and the Gamma parameters, the agreement for the Gamma parameters between the  $ k_{L}/Gamma $  (V4) and Gamma (V3) versions is better than that for the  $ k_{L} $  parameters between the  $ k_{L}/Gamma $  (V4) and  $ k_{L} $  (V2) versions. This also reflected in the standard deviation calculated between all models (last row in Table 9). It can be shown that the  $ k_{L} $  parameters are larger than the Gamma parameters, indicating that the variation of Gamma values between all models are smaller than those of  $ k_{L} $  values. The results reported by Huang et al. $ ^{[25]} $  found that the same Gamma values can be used for the large and small colour-difference datasets. This is not supported by the present results, where the Gamma values between large/small colour differences could differ by a factor of approximately 1.5. This suggests that the Gamma values across the different groups vary according to the magnitude of color differences and surface/display media.

From the  $ k_{L} $  values, the results imply that, for a pair of samples having the same lightness difference  $ (\Delta L^{*}) $ , the difference would appear larger for display colours than for surface colours. $ ^{50-53} $ 

## 5 | CONCLUSION

A comprehensive test to evaluate the performance of 17 colour-difference models using 28 experimental datasets was conducted. The data were divided into three groups: Large Colour-Difference (LCD), Small Colour Difference for surface colours (SCDs), and Small Colour Difference for display colours (SCDd), including 2272, 6601, 4927, sample pairs, respectively. The results

revealed the parametric effects between large/small colour differences, and between surface/display media. Most importantly, the results strongly indicate that the CAM16-UCS uniform colour space gave significantly the most accurate prediction to all types of data. It should be proposed for colour-difference evaluation across all industries. In addition, the  $ k_{L}/Gamma $  parameters of 1.86/0.37, 0.56/0.65, and 0.87/0.56 can be proposed for the SCDs, SCDd, LCD groups, respectively.
